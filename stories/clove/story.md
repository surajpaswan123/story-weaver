The apartment was too quiet. There were no squeaking bedsprings, no distant shouts of other children playing in the halls, no clattering of tin plates from a cafeteria. There was only the empty, heavy silence of an unfamiliar room, broken only by the soft *smack, smack, smack* of my bare feet on the cold, smooth floor. 

I kept one hand pressed flat against the wall, using the smooth plaster to guide me as I paced. I'm only five, too small to reach the light switches, and everything in this room feels giant. At the orphanage, everyone just called me Clove. I don't know who gave me my real name, Clover, or who my parents were, but until a few days ago, I at least knew where I belonged. Now, I am in this strange place that smells like sharp cleaning chemicals and stale dust, and I don't know why I had to be separated from my friends. I just want to go back. 

But I can’t go back. The adults said I was "different." They called it a mutation. 

I remember the smell of rubbing alcohol and latex gloves from the infirmary. I remember the cold metal of the examination table seeping through my thin shirt. The doctors had been speaking in hushed, serious voices. Then, one of them took my arm. I thought he was just going to examine me, but his grip tightened, and with a sudden, forceful twist, there was a sickening *snap*. 

The pain had been a blinding, white-hot flash. I screamed, crying so hard my throat tasted like copper. But the agonizing pain didn't last. Almost instantly, a strange, intense heat flooded my arm. Beneath my skin, it felt like a thousand tiny ants were biting and pulling, knitting the jagged edges of my bone back together. Within five minutes, the throbbing vanished completely. I remember the doctor's fingers probing the spot where the break had just been, his breathing ragged and quick as he confirmed what had happened. I had healed perfectly. 

That wasn't the only thing that made me different. 

As I paced along the wall of this new, quiet apartment, I shivered, shrinking into the massive, heavy weight that currently surrounded me. 

There is something hidden inside my wrists. It’s hard to explain, but I can feel how they work. Whenever I want them to—or whenever I am truly terrified—the skin and muscle on the backs of my wrists seem to just... part. It doesn't hurt. It just feels like a deep, shifting pressure, opening up to let them out. 

Wings. 

But they aren't like bird wings. They aren't made of feathers. As I huddled near the corner of the room, I ran my small fingers through them. They are covered in a dense, incredibly soft fur, as thick and warm as the heaviest winter blanket. And they are huge. I'm so small, and these wings are so impossibly large that just one of them is enough to completely cover my entire body. 

Right now, they are both out. They droop from my wrists, wrapping tightly around my shoulders, my back, and my legs, cocooning me in a dark, furry sphere. I know I can put them away. I can pull them back inside my arms, and my wrists will smooth over until my hands feel exactly like any other little boy's hands. But I don't want to. It is my first time letting them wrap around me like this, and even though having these giant, heavy limbs attached to my arms is deeply scary, the warmth of the fur is the only comforting thing in this strange room. 

I sank down to the floor, my back against the wall, tucking my knees to my chest. The furry wings pulled tighter around me, shielding me from the cold air of the apartment. I buried my face in my knees, listening to the silence. 

The people who brought me here had told me to wait. They said *someone* would be coming for me later. Someone who understood what I was. 

But I didn't want someone new. I wanted the loud, chaotic, familiar smell of the orphanage. I wanted to hear the voices of the other kids. I squeezed my eyes shut, trembling inside my cocoon of fur, feeling completely alone. 

Then, the silence broke. 

It started as a heavy, muffled vibration passing through the floorboards beneath my toes. Footsteps. Out in the hallway. They were slow, deliberate, and unhurried. The vibrations grew stronger, stopping directly on the other side of my front door. 

I held my breath, my small hands gripping the thick fur of my wings. 

A metallic clink echoed through the quiet apartment. It was the sound of a key sliding into the deadbolt. A heavy click followed, and then the slow, drawn-out squeak of the front door swinging open, letting in a draft of cooler hallway air. 

Someone was here.

The heavy scrape of the front door opening sent a cold spike straight through my chest. Heavy boots stepped onto the hardwood of the entryway. My breath hitched. 

*Hide them.* 

It was pure instinct. The doctors back at the orphanage hadn't known about my wings. They had been so entirely focused on my broken arm, so obsessed with feeling my bones knit themselves back together in minutes, that they had completely missed the hidden secrets inside my wrists. I couldn't let this new person find out either. 

I squeezed my eyes shut, focusing hard on my arms. Immediately, a deep, pulling pressure shifted beneath my skin. The massive, dark-furred wings collapsed inward, shrinking and folding at an impossible speed as they slid back into the narrow slits on the backs of my wrists. The heavy, comforting warmth vanished instantly, leaving me shivering violently in the thin cotton t-shirt the orphanage had given me. My skin smoothed over. I grabbed my arms, my small fingers frantically tracing the surface, finding nothing but normal, human wrists again. 

I stayed pressed against the floorboards, curling into the tightest ball I could manage. My chin dug sharply into my kneecaps. I didn't lift my head. I didn't want to hear the sharp click of a doctor laying down another metal tool, or feel the rough hands of an angry adult coming to drag me away to another cold room. My stomach twisted into a tight, painful knot. I just wanted to go back. I wanted my friends. I wanted the familiar, lumpy mattress of my own small cot in the corner of the loud, crowded dormitory. 

"Hello?" I whispered. My voice shook, sounding incredibly tiny in the large, echoing apartment. I swallowed hard, my throat feeling tight, and tried again, just a little louder. "Who is it?" 

I held my breath, keeping my face buried in my knees. I waited for anyone who was there to reply, listening to the heavy silence of the room and the slow, steady rhythm of their breathing. 

For a long moment, the person didn't say anything. The heavy front door swung shut, cutting off the draft from the hallway. I heard the deadbolt slide firmly back into place with a loud, metallic *thunk*. 

Then came the rustle of a thick jacket. A soft, solid thud vibrated against the floorboards, sounding like a heavy canvas bag being dropped by the doorway. 

"I'm sorry it's so dark in here." 

The voice was deep, but it wasn't harsh. It didn't have the sharp, barking edge of the orphanage matrons or the cold, clipped distance of the doctors. It sounded tired, quiet, and surprisingly gentle. The apology about the dark didn't mean anything to me—it was always dark for me—but the calm tone of his voice made me freeze, listening closer. 

Slow, careful footsteps moved across the room. They didn't rush toward me. They stopped a few feet away from where I was huddled in the corner. I still refused to lift my head, my shoulders trembling in the cold. 

"You don't have to look at me if you don't want to," the man said. 

The rustling of heavy fabric told me he was crouching down, bringing himself closer to my level. A new smell drifted over to me, cutting through the sharp, chemical scent of the apartment. It was the smell of damp cotton, cold night air, and maybe a faint trace of peppermint. 

"My name is Elias," he said softly. "The people who brought you here told me your name is Clover. Is that right?" 

I didn't answer. I just shivered, my fingers gripping my elbows so tight my knuckles ached. 

"It's okay to be scared, Clover," Elias continued, his voice staying perfectly calm and steady in the quiet room. "I know today has been really confusing. And I know what happened at the infirmary with the doctors. But you're safe now. I'm not here to run tests on you, and I'm not going to hurt you." 

I slowly shifted, lifting my head just a fraction. Keeping my chin tucked tightly against my knees, I listened intently through the small gap between my arms. I couldn't see the man crouching in front of me, but the sounds and smells painted a clear enough picture. 

Resting on the floorboards, just a little ways away, were his heavy boots. As the man shifted his weight, the leather creaked softly—a dull, worn sound. The thick rubber soles tapped gently against the wood, a muffled vibration that felt completely different from the sharp, echoing *clack-clack* of the shiny, hard dress shoes the doctors always wore. I could smell the damp earth and scuffed, old leather radiating from them. They smelled normal. They smelled like they had walked a long, long way in the rain to get here. 

"I'm not going to grab you," Elias promised, his voice pitching just a little softer, as if he could tell I was analyzing the space between us. "I'm just going to sit right here on the floor for a minute. You can stay in your corner for as long as you need to." 

I heard the heavy rustle of his jacket again, followed by a heavier thud as he shifted from a crouch to sitting directly on the cold hardwood floor. He let out a long, quiet sigh, the kind of breath an adult takes when they are very, very tired. 

He didn't move toward me. He didn't reach out. He just sat there. 

The silence stretched between us, but it felt different now. It wasn't the empty, terrifying isolation from before. It was just quiet.  

A severe shiver wracked through my small frame. Without my furred wings wrapped around me, the drafty chill of the apartment was biting straight through my thin shirt. The floorboards were freezing against my bare feet and legs. I hugged my knees tighter, my teeth clicking together loudly. 

Elias shifted. The sound made me instantly flinch, pulling my chin back down to my knees. 

"It's freezing in here," Elias said. His voice was casual, like he was talking about the weather to a friend, not trying to coax a terrified child out of a corner. I heard the scrape of his heavy boots against the floor, then the metallic purr of a thick zipper sliding open. He was digging into the heavy bag he had dropped by the door.  

I strained my ears, tracking every little noise. There was a soft, muffled sound of fabric being pulled loose.  

"I brought a blanket," Elias murmured. "It's clean. Very soft. I'm going to slide it across the floor toward you, okay? You don't have to take it if you don't want to. But it's there if you're cold." 

I held my breath. A gentle, sweeping swish whispered against the smooth wood. A moment later, a bundle of fabric bumped softly against my bare toes.  

I didn't move right away. I waited to hear if Elias was going to lunge forward while I was distracted. But his breathing remained steady, anchored firmly to the same spot a few feet away.  

Slowly, my trembling fingers reached out from the tight ball of my body. My fingertips brushed against the bundle. It was a thick, heavy fleece. It didn't smell like the sterile bleach of the orphanage or the harsh cleaners of the apartment. It smelled like fresh laundry soap and warm air. 

I gripped the edge of the fleece and pulled it toward me in one quick, desperate jerk. I threw it over my head, letting the heavy fabric drape completely over my shivering shoulders and back, cocooning myself in the dark just like I had done with my wings earlier. The warmth was immediate, trapping my own body heat against my skin.  

Under the safety of the heavy blanket, I let out a shaky, stuttering breath.  

"Better?" Elias asked gently.  

I gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod beneath the blanket, though I still didn't speak. I didn't know this man. I didn't know why I was here. But as I huddled in the corner under the warm fleece, listening to the quiet, steady rhythm of his breathing, the painful knot in my stomach began to loosen just a fraction.

"I came here to give you some groceries," Elias said. The sound of thick paper bags shifting and crinkling inside the heavy canvas duffel accompanied his words. "And your caretaker will be here soon. She just needs to finish filling out some paperwork."

*My caretaker.* 

The words cut right through the freezing panic in my chest. I perked up, slowly lifting my head from my knees. Beneath the heavy, warm canopy of the fleece blanket, my memory suddenly clicked into place. The deep, tired gravel of his voice. The slow, heavy footsteps. I knew this man. Beneath the smell of cold rain and wet leather, there was a faint, familiar trace of industrial floor wax and pine soap lingering on his clothes. 

Uncle Elias. The orphanage janitor. He was the man who always warned me when the hallway floors were wet so I wouldn't slip, the one who let me listen to his giant ring of brass keys jingling against his hip so I knew exactly where he was standing. 

"Uncle Elias?" My voice wavered, small and tight in the echoing room.

"Yeah, buddy. It's me," he answered softly, the floorboards groaning a little as he shifted his weight.

The familiar connection crumbled the last of my brave walls. "I want to go back home," I cried, my voice breaking. I clutched the thick edges of the fleece blanket in my small fists, pulling it tighter around my neck. "I'm so scared!"

He didn't shush me. He just let me cry for a moment, the heavy, steady rhythm of his breathing a constant anchor in the room. 

My mind raced back to what he had just said. *My caretaker will be here.* Elly was coming. A massive, overwhelming wave of relief washed over me. When I was first brought to the orphanage as a baby, Elly had been the one to take me in. She was only eighteen back then, and now I was five, but she had always treated me like I was her own. She was the one who held my hand and taught me how to count my steps down the corridors so I wouldn't bump into the walls. Her apron always smelled like warm milk and lavender lotion. If Elly was coming, everything would be okay. 

But Uncle Elias let out a heavy, tired sigh, and the sound brought me crashing back to the cold floor of the apartment. There were more pressing matters right now.

"I know it's difficult, Clover," Elias said, his tone turning serious, though it stayed incredibly gentle. "But you have to understand. Some of the people back there... they don't want you in that building anymore. Not after what they saw."

I stopped crying, holding my breath to listen. 

"If you go back," Elias continued, his voice tightening with worry, "what if those doctors come back to take you away again? They'll just keep locking you in that infirmary. They'll keep breaking your bones, over and over, just to watch them heal. Do you want that?"

My right hand instantly shot to my left arm. My small fingers wrapped tightly around the spot where the doctor had snapped it in half just hours ago. The phantom echo of that searing, sharp agony flared in my memory, followed immediately by the terrifying, swarming heat of my mutation tearing through my muscle to knit the bone back together. 

I didn't understand the rules that big people made. The adults were always making arbitrary lines, separating kids, moving us from room to room, whispering about things that made no sense. It was all so confusing and annoying. Why couldn't I just stay in my own bed? Why did being different mean I wasn't allowed to go home?

But pain wasn't confusing. Pain was simple. I never, ever wanted to feel my arm snap like a dry branch again. 

"No," I whispered, shrinking deeper into my dark fleece cocoon. 

"Then you have to stay here," Elias said firmly. "Just for a little while. Just until Elly gets here and we can figure out a safe place for you to go."

I didn't argue. I just kept my hand clamped over my arm, tracing the smooth, perfectly unbroken skin where the fracture used to be. The quiet of the apartment didn't feel quite as empty anymore, knowing Uncle Elias was sitting right there, guarding the door. 

The heavy canvas bag rustled again. "You haven't eaten since lunch, have you?" he asked. 

I shook my head beneath the blanket, then remembered he couldn't see my nod through the thick fabric. "No," I mumbled. 

"I brought a few things from the kitchens before I left. Nothing fancy, but it'll fill your stomach." 

I heard the sharp crinkle of a plastic wrapper, followed by a soft, sliding sound against the hardwood. A moment later, something small and light bumped gently against my bare toes right at the edge of the blanket. 

I hesitated for a second, listening to make sure he was still sitting a few feet away. His breathing hadn't moved. Slowly, cautiously, I reached one hand out from beneath the warm fleece. My fingers patted the cold floorboards until they brushed against a smooth, rectangular package. A granola bar. 

I pulled it quickly into my cocoon. My hands shook a little as I tore the plastic open. The sweet smell of honey and oats filled the small space beneath the blanket, making my stomach rumble violently in response. I took a small bite, the dry oats crunching loudly in my ears, and for the first time since the doctors dragged me into the infirmary, I felt like I could take a full breath.

"Take your time," Elias murmured quietly, the floorboards creaking as he stretched his legs out. "We've got nowhere to be. We just wait for Elly."

I took my time with the granola bar. I sat with my knees pulled up to my chest, completely hidden beneath the heavy drape of the fleece blanket, and held the small rectangular block with both hands. It was sticky with honey, the dry oats rough against my tongue, and it was the best thing I had ever tasted. I chewed as slowly as I could, letting the sweet flavor fill my mouth, terrified that once I finished the last bite, the terrifying reality of the empty, chemical-smelling apartment would come crashing back down on me. 

Little crumbs tumbled down my chin and bounced off my thin cotton shirt, landing on my bare legs. I didn't care. The thick fleece trapped my body heat, turning my small, dark corner of the floor into a warm, safe cocoon. For the first time since the doctor had grabbed my arm, the violent shivering in my shoulders had finally stopped.

I listened to the slow, steady rhythm of Uncle Elias breathing a few feet away. Every now and then, his heavy boots would scrape lightly against the hardwood as he shifted his weight, or his thick jacket would rustle. He didn't try to talk to me while I ate. He just sat guard between me and the front door, leaving me alone in the quiet.

But the quiet left too much room for me to think. 

*Elly is coming,* I told myself, swallowing a dry mouthful of oats. The thought should have made me feel completely better, but a cold, heavy lump of worry was starting to form in the bottom of my stomach. 

Elly was my caretaker. She was the one who tied my shoes, the one who smelled like lavender lotion and warm milk, and the one who always made sure the bigger kids didn't push me in the cafeteria line. But Elly worked at the orphanage. She had her own small room at the end of the second-floor hallway. She had a life there. She had thirty other kids to look after, to make sure they brushed their teeth and made their beds. 

Why would she come to this strange, freezing apartment? 

Was she going to stay here with me? Or was she just coming to visit? Maybe she was just bringing my clothes. The adults at the orphanage were always moving kids around, packing them into cars, taking them to new places, and then leaving them there. What if Elly was just coming to say goodbye? 

The thought made my throat tighten so painfully I couldn't swallow the last bite of my food. I held the sticky piece of granola in my fist, my heart starting to thump hard against my ribs. I couldn't live in this apartment by myself. I was only five. I couldn't even reach the door handles properly. If Elly left me here, I would be all alone.

"Uncle?" I whispered. My voice was muffled by the thick layer of fleece draped over my head, trembling in the quiet room.

The heavy floorboards groaned immediately as Elias shifted, his jacket rustling. "What is it, buddy?" he answered, his voice a low, gentle rumble. 

I swallowed hard, my fingers curling tightly into the warm blanket. "When is she coming?"

A long, heavy silence stretched between us. I heard Elias let out a slow, tired breath, the kind that made his massive chest hitch just a little. 

"No idea," he said honestly. 

My chest squeezed. I squeezed my eyes shut, pressing my face into my knees. 

"I'm sorry, Clover," Elias continued, his voice softening even more. "I know waiting is the hardest part. But sneaking out of that building isn't easy. Elly has to make sure the director thinks she's just going home for the night. She has to sign out, pack a few things without anyone asking questions, and make sure she isn't followed. That takes time."

"Is she..." I stopped, my voice catching. I took a shaky breath, trying to force the words out. "Is she going to go back? After she comes here?"

The rustling of his jacket stopped completely. The air in the room felt perfectly still for a second. 

"No," Elias said. The word was firm. Absolute. There was no hesitation in his voice at all. "No, she isn't going back, Clover. She's staying with you."

"But she has a job," I argued weakly, picking at a crumb on my knee. "She has the other kids."

"She loves you, kiddo," Elias murmured. "More than that job. More than that building. When they took you to the infirmary today... when they separated you from the others..." He paused, and I heard the faint, metallic jingle of his brass keys as he adjusted his posture on the floor. "She wasn't going to let them keep you. Neither was I. As soon as she found out what the doctors did to your arm, she told me to bring you here. She's leaving the orphanage, Clover. She's coming to take care of you."

A massive, breathless weight lifted off my chest. I slumped slightly against the wall behind me, my shoulders dropping as the tight knot in my stomach finally unspooled. She was staying. She wasn't just dropping me off. 

I finished the last bite of the granola bar, chewing quickly now, the sweet taste of honey returning. 

"Here," Elias said. The heavy canvas bag beside him crinkled loudly. I heard the sharp *crack* of a plastic seal breaking, followed by the twisting of a plastic cap. "You need to drink something. You've been crying a lot."

A smooth, round bottle bumped gently against my toes at the edge of the blanket. I reached out, my fingers finding the cold, thin plastic. I pulled it back inside my cocoon. It was water. I hadn't realized how painfully dry my throat was until I took the first sip. I ended up gulping down half the bottle in seconds, the cool water washing away the sticky sweetness of the oats. 

"Slow down," Elias chuckled warmly, a low, rumbling sound that vibrated slightly through the floor. "You'll make your stomach hurt."

I lowered the bottle, wiping my mouth with the back of my hand. I felt... okay. My arm still carried a faint, phantom ache where the bone had snapped, and my wrists felt unusually heavy, pulsing with the deep, hidden pressure of the massive furred wings folded tightly beneath my skin. But the shivering was gone. The paralyzing terror of the infirmary was fading, replaced by the warm, thick smell of the fleece blanket and the steady presence of Uncle Elias.

"Where are we?" I asked quietly, keeping the blanket draped securely over my head. 

"It's a safe house," Elias replied. I heard the faint rustle of a paper bag being folded up. "Just an old, empty apartment on the edge of the city. A friend of mine lets me use it when I need to fix things. The director at the orphanage doesn't know about it. The doctors don't know about it. Nobody is going to come looking for you here."

I pressed my ear against my knee, listening past the sound of his voice. He was right. We weren't near the orphanage anymore. The orphanage was surrounded by tall brick walls and quiet, empty roads. Here, if I listened very closely, I could feel a faint, distant rumble vibrating through the walls of the apartment. It was a heavy, continuous thrum—the sound of large trucks driving on a busy road miles away. Occasionally, the faint, high-pitched wail of a siren would echo through the plaster, so distant it sounded like a ghost. We were somewhere deep inside a loud, busy place. 

"Are you going to stay too?" I asked. 

"For a little while," Elias said. "I need to make sure Elly gets here safely. And I need to talk to her about where you two are going to go next."

"We can't stay here?"

"Not forever," he answered patiently. "This place doesn't have a bed for you, for one thing. And there's no heat. It's just a place to hide for the night. Once Elly gets here, we'll figure out a real plan. Somewhere you can have a room, and a backyard, and..." He trailed off, sighing softly. "Somewhere you can just be a kid. Without anyone poking and prodding at you."

I rested my chin on my knees, hugging the water bottle to my chest. "Because I'm different," I murmured. 

"Because you're special," Elias corrected immediately, his voice completely serious. "There's a difference, Clover. The people in that infirmary... they're scared of things they don't understand. They look at you, and they see a puzzle they want to take apart. But you aren't a puzzle. You're a five-year-old boy. And nobody has the right to hurt you just because you heal fast."

I touched my left arm again, my fingertips tracing the smooth, unbroken skin. I thought about the heavy, shifting pressure in my wrists. I hadn't told him about the wings. I hadn't told anyone. When the doctors broke my arm, they had been so focused on the bone that they never noticed the strange slits hidden in my skin. 

Did Elias know? Did Elly? 

I squeezed the water bottle, the plastic crinkling loudly in the quiet room. If I told him, would he change his mind? Would he look at me like the doctors did? Even though I couldn't see, I knew what that kind of fear sounded like. I knew how adults breathed when they were terrified—short, sharp, shallow gasps. I didn't want Uncle Elias to breathe like that. I didn't want him to leave me in this cold room. 

So, I kept my wrists tucked tightly against my chest, locking the heavy, dark fur away beneath my skin. 

"I'm tired," I whispered, the exhaustion of the day finally catching up to me. My eyes felt heavy, and the warm cocoon of the fleece blanket was making my limbs feel like lead. 

"I know, buddy," Elias said. I heard him shift, leaning his back against the wall near the door. The floorboards settled under his heavy weight. "Go to sleep. I'll sit right here. I'm not going to leave the door, and I'll wake you up the second Elly turns the key in the lock."

"Promise?" my voice was barely more than a squeak. 

"I promise," Elias vowed, the deep rumble of his voice wrapping around me like a second blanket. "Nobody is getting through this door, Clover. You're safe."

I let out a long, shaky breath. I laid down on my side, pulling my knees up and curling into a tight ball on the hard floor. I wrapped the thick fleece entirely around myself, tucking the edges beneath my body so not a single draft of cold air could reach me. I kept the empty water bottle near my hands, and I kept my left arm guarded against my chest. 

Listening to the faint, distant rumble of the city, and the slow, protective rhythm of Uncle Elias breathing by the door, I closed my eyes and let the darkness finally pull me under, waiting for the smell of lavender and warm milk to come home.

I lay perfectly still on the hard, flat floorboards, listening to the quiet of the apartment. Uncle Elias had just told me there was no bed here. He didn’t know my secret. He didn’t know that I had the best bed in the world hidden right inside my arms. 

I traced a finger over the smooth skin on the back of my left wrist. If I wasn’t so terribly afraid of scaring him—of hearing him gasp with that sharp, terrified breath the doctors always made—I would have let my wings out right then. My massive, furred wings were thicker, warmer, and softer than the very best mattress at the orphanage. I could have folded them beneath me and wrapped them over my top, sleeping inside a perfect, heavy cloud of my own making. I was certain it would be more comfortable than anything else in the world. 

But I kept them locked away beneath my skin, choosing the cold floor and the fleece blanket instead. 

*Elly will be coming soon,* I told myself, repeating the thought like a protective shield. She was coming, and she was going to stay. 

But as the silence stretched on, my mind wouldn't stop spinning. The questions of a five-year-old kept bubbling up, replacing the fear with a quiet, nagging worry. Uncle Elias said this place was just an empty safe house. He said there was no bed. But what about other things? He had brought a heavy canvas bag full of groceries, but how were we going to eat them? Was there even a stove in this place? Did we have pots, or pans, or plates? I sniffed the air beneath my blanket. The apartment only smelled like sharp floor cleaner and stale dust. It didn't smell like a place where people cooked or lived. 

Thinking all this, my eyelids grew incredibly heavy. The exhaustion of the day—the searing pain of the infirmary, the terror of the healing, the confusing escape—finally caught up to me all at once. The distant, continuous rumble of the city outside faded into a dull hum. Uncle Elias’s slow, steady breathing sitting by the door became a rhythmic lullaby. My grip on the empty plastic water bottle loosened, and I fell fast asleep. 

***

I didn't know how much time had passed when I finally stirred. 

At first, the world was just a thick, fuzzy haze. My head felt heavy, wrapped tightly in the disorienting fog of deep sleep. I was still curled on my side, my cheek pressed against the smooth, cold floorboards, completely bundled in the thick fleece blanket. 

I kept my eyes closed, drifting somewhere between dreaming and waking. But something had changed in the room. The absolute, heavy silence from before was gone. 

I was hearing two people talking. 

Their voices were muffled and low, deliberately hushed so they wouldn't echo too loudly in the empty space. Through the thick layer of my fleece blanket and the lingering fog of sleep, the sounds blended together like a quiet hum. 

Slowly, my mind began to clear. I lay perfectly still, holding my breath to listen. 

One voice was the deep, vibrating rumble I already knew—Uncle Elias. 

The other voice was lighter, breathless, and trembling with a frantic, desperate kind of worry. It was a woman's voice. 

I inhaled deeply, my nose twitching. Cutting straight through the sterile, chemical smell of the apartment was a sudden, beautiful draft of air. It was a scent that instantly made my throat tighten with relief. Damp wool from the foggy night outside. The faint, sweet smell of warm milk. And lavender lotion. 

Elly. 

"Did they hurt him?" Elly's voice trembled, breaking through my sleep-fog. She sounded like she had been running. "Elias, tell me the truth. When I went to the infirmary, the director wouldn't let me inside. They just kept saying there was an incident. Did they hurt my boy?"

"Keep your voice down, El," Elias murmured gently, his heavy boots shifting on the floorboards. "He's asleep right over there."

There was a sudden rustle of fabric, and the sharp, frantic intake of Elly's breath. "Oh, God. He’s on the floor. Is he okay?"

"He's terrified," Elias answered, his deep voice heavy with a tired sadness. "They broke his arm, Elly. Clean through the bone. Just to see what his body would do."

I heard Elly let out a shattered, muffled sob. The sound of her crying sent a jolt of panic straight through my chest. I didn't want Elly to be sad. 

"It's healed," Elias quickly reassured her. "You know how his mutation works. By the time I managed to sneak him out the back corridor, the bone was already knitted back together. He isn't physically hurt anymore. But he was shaking so badly when we got here... he thought you were just coming to say goodbye."

"Never," Elly whispered fiercely, her voice thick with tears. "I packed whatever I could carry. I left my keys on the director's desk. I am never letting them touch him again."
